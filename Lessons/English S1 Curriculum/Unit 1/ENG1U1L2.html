Purpose

Sometimes a collective is considered one entity and is used as a singular noun, while at other times it is considered plural. You must be able to recognize which it is in order to pair it with the correct form of the verb.

"To be countable or not to be countable, that is the question..."

In this lesson we will examine collective, count, and non-count nouns.

Parts of Speech

Overview
Noun

A word that refers to a person, place, thing, event, substance, or quality

'Doctor', 'shoes', and 'headphones' are all nouns.

An example of a countable noun is 'table', and an example of an uncountable noun is 'money'.

Pronouns

A word that is used instead of a noun or a noun phrase

"She", "it", and "who" are all examples of pronouns.

Articles

Any of the English words "a", "an", and "the", or words in other languages that do the same job as these.

When I say "I'm a dentist", I am using the indefinite article "a".

Adjectives

A word that describes a noun or pronoun

"Big", "boring", "purple", and "obvious" are all adjectives.

Adverbs

A word that describes or gives more information about a verb, adjective, adverb, or phrase

In the phrase "she smiled cheerfully", the word "cheerfully" is an adverb.

Verbs

A a word or phrase that describes an action, condition, or experience

The words "run", "keep", and "feel" are all verbs.

Conjunction

A word such as 'and', 'but', 'while', or 'although' that connects words, phrases, and clauses in a sentence

The conjunction "although" joins the two clauses in the sentence "He left, although I begged him not to."

Prepositions

In grammar, a word that is used before a noun, a noun phrase, or a pronoun, connecting it to another word

In the sentences "We jumped in the lake", and "She drove slowly down the track", "in" and "down" are prepositions.

Collective Nouns

A noun is a word that refers to a person, place or thing. It can also refer to an abstract idea such as pain, intelligence, or love. Although nouns are relatively simple to understand, there are a few instances that pose problems to the writer.

A collective noun is a word used to define a group of things like a herd of cows. Although a herd is considered one item, it is composed of more than one thing. That's where the trouble begins for a writer.

Examples:

  • The class is going on a field trip to see the capital building.
  • The class must begin their assignments soon or they will receive a failing grade.

In one instance the class is considered singular and in the other it is considered plural. Can you see the difference?

In the first sentence, the class is acting as one because they are all going on the field trip. However, in the second sentence, although they must all begin their assignments, they are working individually on something different and maybe even at different times.

If collective nouns are troublesome, you can do one of two things to ensure correct usage. First, you might insert the word 'members' or 'players' in, for words like jury, committee, team. That way you can use a plural verb safely.

  • Team members were disappointed at their loss.

Second, you can substitute an entirely different word such as birds instead of flock, or soldiers instead of army.

  • The soldiers marched proudly past.

Count Nouns vs. Non-count Nouns

Count Nouns
Non-Count Nouns

Count nouns refer to things that can be counted as individual units or separate parts. They usually refer to what can be seen or heard. Some examples are:

  • Bottle
  • Finger
  • Table
  • Chair
  • Girl
  • Boy

These nouns normally refer to something more abstract, such as a concept or a word that has a collective meaning. Non-count nouns cannot be separated. They are thought of as a whole unit. Some examples are:

  • Furniture
  • Anger
  • Education
  • Weather
  • Clothing
  • Grass

Think of water. Water is liquid and cannot be divided into parts; it is a non-count noun. However, if you freeze it in ice cube trays, each cube can be counted, making it a count noun. Think of non-count nouns as the butter, a mass that cannot be separated. Think of the baked cake as the count nouns, which can be cut into individual pieces.

Countable Nouns Uncountable Nouns

Now that we know what count and non-count nouns are, let's learn how to use them correctly when we are writing.

The Rules

The plural of count nouns is acquired by adding an "s" on the end of each word.

For example:

  • I ate one apple on Monday.
  • I ate two apples on Wednesday.
  • I read this book two years ago.
  • I bought those books at a garage sale.

Why Do I Need to Know

Here are a few examples.

The real problem for writers when using count and non-count nouns is using the correct modifiers, such as:

  • Amount/number
  • Little/few
  • Less/fewer
  • Much/many

For example, in the following sentence, which of the choices is correct?

I am going to try to eat (less/fewer) calories so I will lose weight.

Step 1: To decide which is correct, you have to look at the noun being modified and decide whether it is a count noun or a non-count noun. In this case, the noun is calories. Can you count calories? Sure, you can.

Step 2: The next step is to decide which modifier is correct. You can divide certain modifiers into count and non-count modifiers, too. Words like amount, little, less, and much have more to do with how much, while number, few, fewer, and many have to do with how many.

Conclusion: The answer above, then, is fewer calories.

Consider the following:

  • She was (less/fewer) than 60 years old.

In this case, years refers to a period of time rather than individual years, so the answer is less.

Nouns vs. Noun Phrases

While a noun is the single person, place, thing, or idea, a noun phrase consists of the noun and any other words modifying it.
For example, consider the following sentence: My older brother wants to become a chef.
"Brother" is a noun and serves as the sentence’s subject. "My older brother" is the entire noun phrase. "My" and "older" modify the noun "brother".

Noun Clauses

If a noun can be a single word or phrase, why can’t it be an entire clause?
For example, consider the following sentence: This shirt is too big.
"Shirt" is a noun and serves as the subject of a sentence.

Now, look at this sentence:
What I bought at the store is too big.
"What I bought at the store" is a noun clause because the entire clause serves as a noun, the subject of the sentence. "I" is the subject of the noun clause and "bought" is the verb.
