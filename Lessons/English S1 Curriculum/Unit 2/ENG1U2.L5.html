LESSON KEYWORDS

HUMAN RIGHTS

Human rights are the basic rights that every person is entitled to just because they are human.

LEGAL RIGHTS

Legal rights are the rights that are recognized and protected by the law.

MORAL RIGHTS

Moral rights are rights that are based on ethical or moral principles.

SOCIAL JUSTICE

Social justice is the idea that everyone should have equal rights and opportunities, regardless of their race, gender, religion, or other social group.

REFLEXIONS:

Imagine someone says: "Killing is wrong in most cases." You might disagree and believe that the death penalty should be enforced in certain situations. To explain your answer, you could say that you think the death penalty can serve as a deterrent for severe crimes and can provide justice for victims and their families. However, you might also pose the question: "What if the person being punished is innocent? Should we consider the possibility of wrongful execution?" This can lead to a deeper discussion about the complex issue of the death penalty and the value of human life.

Every individual has the natural right listed in this image, and there are several documents that outline these including the U.S. Bill of Rights and the Universal Declaration of Human Rights adopted by the United Nations.

Now, let's say the statement is: "People should be allowed to say or write whatever they wish." You might have some doubts about this. You might ask: "What if they are writing hate speech or spreading harmful ideas? Does that mean we should allow it? What if they are saying something hurtful or offensive about someone you care about, like your mom? Should they still be allowed to say it?" Asking these questions can help you think more critically about the issue of free speech and whether there are limits to it. You could also consider the counterargument: "What if we weren't allowed to express ourselves freely? How would that affect our ability to share ideas and opinions?" This can lead to a meaningful discussion about the importance of free expression and how to balance it with the need for respect and responsibility.

WHAT ARE HUMAN RIGHTS
AND WHO DECIDES:

According to www.amnestyusa.org:

The word “rights” is used in a number of different contexts to include legal, moral, and human rights. These rights which are thought to have universal application are known as human rights.

Legal rights are laid down by law. Some countries (such as the U.S.) have a Bill of Rights. In others, such rights are written into their Constitutions. In others (such as the UK), legal rights are not written down in either a Constitution or a Bill of Rights; instead it is assumed that a person has the right to do something until a ruling to the contrary has been made by a court or by Parliament.

Moral Rights are often taken to mean rights which are not enshrined in any formal code but which nonetheless are held to be reasonable; some examples of this might be the right to privacy or the right to confidentiality.

The basics rights and freedoms to which all humans are entitled, often held to include the right to life and liberty, freedom of thought and expression, and equality before the law.

Moral rights are principles or values that are considered to be fundamental to a fair and just society. They are often related to the inherent dignity and worth of all human beings, and are considered to be universal and applicable to all people, regardless of their cultural, social, or economic background.
