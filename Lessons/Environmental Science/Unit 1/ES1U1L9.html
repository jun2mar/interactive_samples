Increasing Water Supplies

In other words, we can’t increase the total amount of water present on a global basis, but we can increase the amount of water available to a local town, human population center, or other region. We’ll look at a few ways of increasing the water supply on the next few pages.

Desalination

Remember that most of all water on Earth is saltwater found in oceans and seas. If humans could remove the salt from the ocean we would be left with drinkable freshwater.

Desalination is the process of removing salt from seawater, and it is a promising new technology for increasing local freshwater supplies. This process has been used in many locales in California and also in El Paso, Texas, to increase the local supply of suitable freshwater in the face of a booming human population and an otherwise limited freshwater supply.

There are several ways to accomplish desalination, but the most promising has been the use of reverse osmosis. You may even have a reverse osmosis water filter on your sink or your refrigerator. Municipalities use the same scientific innovation on a larger scale. Reverse osmosis uses a biochemistry technology that was discovered long ago in living cells. The idea of a semi-permeable membrane is that only some select substances can pass through the membrane. The shape and size of the gaps within the membrane dictate what substances can pass through. On a micro-technology scale, scientists have created a semi-permeable membrane through which they can force sea water at very high pressures. The water passes through the membrane while the particulate matter, including the salts, is left behind.

Desalination Drawbacks

There are several problems associated with desalination. Two obvious limitations to desalination as a way to increase water supplies are that the population must be close to the ocean and that there is a large amount of salt that is removed from the water, which is essentially waste. Other drawbacks are that the process is highly expensive and requires large inputs of energy.

Desalination, continued

Even though there are drawbacks to desalination, many coastal populations use the process to increase freshwater supplies. Worldwide there are more than 13,000 desalination plants producing more than 12 billion gallons of water a day. The largest desalination plant in the world is the Jebel Ali Plant in the United Arab Emirates, and the largest in the United States is in Tampa Bay, Florida. The Tampa Bay plant can produce more than 25 million gallons of freshwater every day.


Tampa skyline

An interesting feature of the Tampa Bay desalination plant is that it sits next to a power plant. The salt removed from seawater at the desalination plant is diluted with cooling water from the power plant and then returned to the sea (Tampa Bay). Scientists say that this process of diluting the salt with cooling water means that it does not cause environmental harm when it is returned to the waters of Tampa Bay.

Unfortunately, not all plants dilute the salt before discharging it back into the source water. If not done carefully, the return of the extra salt back to the source water can harm fish and other marine organisms.

Transporting Water

Another means of increasing a local area’s water supply is to transport water from places of water excess to places of water deficit. Pipelines and canals, sometimes many hundred miles long, have been built to move the water from one place to another. An example of this can be seen in California. The southern part of the state is very dry and supports a large population, while the northern part of the state receives a lot of rainfall and has a relatively small population. The excess water in Northern California is piped to Southern California to help meet the demand for water.

Dams

Dams are built to control the flow of water, so water can be saved in times of excess, and released in times of greater need. A dam is a barrier constructed to retain water until a specified time at which the water is released, as seen in the photo. The most famous dam in the United States is the Hoover Dam, which was built on the Colorado River between Nevada and Arizona.

Increasing Water Supplies by Practicing Conservation

Conservation means using a resource carefully and efficiently so that it will always be available and suitable for use. So if humans use and waste less water, the amount of available water will increase exponentially. Over the next set of slides you will learn how water can be conserved in the agricultural, industrial, and domestic sectors.

Conserving Agricultural Water

About 60 to 70% of agricultural water never reaches its target crop. Instead it flows over the ground before the crops absorb it or it evaporates before it can be put to good use. If we can develop more efficient means of irrigating crops we can save large amounts of water. One such technology is drip irrigation, which delivers a measured amount of water exactly to where the plants need it and can use it. The picture shows you how this works.

Water Conservation in Industry

According to the US Geological Survey, industry and electricity generation accounts for over 40% of US freshwater use, but most of this is non-consumptive use. This is good news. It means that most of the water used in industry can be recycled within a factory. Industries can still do more, however, to conserve water, and one of the biggest ways to ensure this would be to increase the price of water, so that people and agricultural entities would be less likely to abuse the water supply.

Of course, this is controversial, but it also would provide incentives for the development of new manufacturing processes that use water more carefully. Another way that water can be conserved in industry is by decreasing the amount of air, land, and water pollution produced by power plants and factories. By limiting the amount of pollution, Earth’s available water supplies will be cleaner and safer for those in need.

Water Conservation in Homes

This little room in every American house is where the majority of domestic water is consumed. Public use of water accounts for about 13% of all US water use. The single biggest consumer of water in US homes is toilet flushing. The US government has set standards that require new toilets that use no more than 1.6 gallons per flush. Other things that can be done to conserve household water include fixing leaks, using low-flow shower heads, and making sure that lawn watering is done efficiently.

Think About This!

A low-flow shower head can save about 5,000 gallons of water per person every year. There are approximately 300 million people in America.

Raising the Price of Water

What would you do if your favorite store had a huge sale? You might be tempted to buy a lot, right? The first lesson in this course mentioned that one cause of environmental problems is that we undervalue our resources. This is certainly the case for water. Many American towns charge one low flat rate for water use. In other words, people who consume high amounts of water are charged the same amount as those who use very little water—thus providing no financial incentive for people to conserve water. Scientists have seen that for every 10% increase in water prices, water use goes down 3 to 7%.

In order to avoid hurting the poor, one idea related to this concept is to charge very little or nothing for a lifetime quantity of water, but then increase the price in steps as a person uses more and more of it. This way everyone, even the poor, would get the basic amount of water they need to live for a very low cost. Use of water above the basic amount needed to live would then incur a higher cost. As you review this lesson and go forward in this course, continue to think about ways that you impact the Earth both positively and negatively. Many times awareness is the difference between helping and hurting the planet.
